PET EMERGENCY SITUATION AND WHAT TO DO

2020-OGUNDAIRO SAMUEL

What should you do when there's an emergency? Nobody wishes for it to happen, however, we cannot shy away from the fact that emergencies does happen.

So, what do you do incase of an emergency? There are different types of emergencies for pets. It can be poisoning, difficulty in breathing attack by another animal, sudden illness or being hit by a car, whatever the situation is, knowing what to do is very crucial in that kind of situation. Let take a look at few of the most common ones:

Poisoning: first of all, once its noticed, determine what and how much your pet has ingested and seek the vet immediately. Information about what was taken will help the vet.

Accidents: accidents involving vehicle accidents with your pet can cause trauma. The first step is to get it out of the way and cover it with towel or box while heading straight to the vet.

Breathing difficulty: if the item ingested can be seen and dislodged, do so. If it’s not visible, head straight to your vet immediately.

Allergies and other medical conditions are best treated with vets. If you are a pet-sitter, be sure to have the owner’s contact handy. They might know what to do and what to give it to remedy the situation incase of allergies.


HOW LONG DOES POISON STAY IN A DOG'S SYSTEM

2020-OGUNDAIRO SAMUEL

It's widespread for pets such as cats and dogs to be exposed to hazardous substances in the environment. The USA, the country with the largest companion animal population in the world (72 million pet dogs), recorded over two hundred thousand cases of dog poisoning in 2019 alone.

Dogs are notoriously curious explorers, and this makes them vulnerable to contact with toxic substances. Dogs could be poisoned through contact with, ingestion, or inhalation of hazardous materials.

Typically, the amount of time poison stays in a dog's system depends on factors such as the type and amount of poison they've encountered, the treatment methods, and how quickly these treatments are administered.

Most people assume "poison" refers to just chemical substances, but medication and food which are necessary for humans may be harmful to dogs. Ingested poisons include chocolate, human medicines, grapes, raisins, pesticides, poisoned rodents, or cleaning substances; inhaled toxins include insecticides, carbon monoxide and examples of contact poison are tar, paint, and arsenic.
